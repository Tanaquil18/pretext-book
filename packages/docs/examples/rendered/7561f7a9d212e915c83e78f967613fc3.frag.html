Subsection 1.1

Question 1.1.1. Unions of vector subspaces.

Is the union of subspaces of a vector space always a vector space?

Hint.

Examples are often the key to answering questions like this. Pick a nice vector space like \(\mathbb R^2\) or \(\mathbb R^3\) and play.

Answer.

No they are not. For example, the \(x\)-axis and \(y\)-axis in \(\mathbb R^2\) are both subspaces, but their union is not since the union is not closed under addition: \((0,1) + (1,0) = (1,1)\text{,}\) and the vector \((1,1)\) is not a member of either axis.

Solution.

Show that if \(U,W\) are subspaces of a vector space \(V\text{,}\) then \(U\cup W\) is a subspace if and only if \(U\subseteq W\) or \(W\subseteq U.\)
